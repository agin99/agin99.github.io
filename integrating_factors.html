2/16/25: Solving ODEs with integrating factors

ODEs using the Integrating Factor Method

Ordinary differential equations (ODEs) are equations that include an unknown function of a single independent variable and its derivatives. The equation relates the rate of change to the unknown function itself and the goal is to use mathematical techniques to simplify the equation form and solve for the unknown function. This blog will be about an analytical method that uses an integrating factor to simplify the equation into a form friendly to direct integration.

Using the Differentiation Product Rule

To begin, we’ll show that an equality of the form $$ \frac{df}{dt} g(t) + f(t) \frac{dg}{dt} = Q(t) g(t) $$ with \(g(t)\) and \(Q(t)\) as known functions can be solved for an unknown function \(f(t)\). The product rule for differentiation allows us to rewrite the left side of the equality as $$ \frac{df}{dt} g(t) + f(t) \frac{dg}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}[f(t)g(t)] $$ where \(t\) is the single independent variable. Using the product rule, we cast the original equality as $$ \frac{d}{dt}[f(t)g(t)] = Q(t) g(t) $$ Finally, applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus of Calculus to integrate both sides, $$ \begin{align} \int \frac{d}{dt}[f(t)g(t)] &= \int Q(t) g(t) dt \\ f(t)g(t) &= \int Q(t) g(t) dt \\ f(t) &= \frac{1}{g(t)} \int Q(t) g(t) dt \end{align} $$ Since \(g(t)\) and \(Q(t)\) are known functions of the independent variable \(t\), the unknown \(f(t)\) is a solvable through integration by parts.

Casting an ODE into the Standard Form

Given the above proof for finding the unknown \(f(t)\), the question of whether or not to use the integrating factor method becomes a dependent on if the ODE can be algebraically manipulated into the form $$ \frac{df}{dt} + P(t)f(t) = Q(t) $$ If we can cast the ODE in this way, the goal is to find a multiplicative factor that can be used across the entire equality, \(g(t)\), such that $$ \frac{df}{dt} g(t) + f(t) \frac{dg}{dt} = Q(t) g(t) $$ for \(\frac{dg}{dt} = P(t)g(t)\). This further offsets the goal to one of solving the differential equation \(\frac{dg}{dt} = P(t)g(t)\), which has a solution of \(g(t) = e^{\int P(t) dt}\). With the integrating factor, \(g(t)\), we may write the algebraically manipulated equality as $$ \begin{align} g(t)\frac{df}{dt} + g(t)P(t)f(t) &= g(t)Q(t) \\ e^{\int P(t) dt} \frac{df}{dt} + P(t) e^{\int P(t) dt} f(t) &= e^{\int P(t) dt} Q(t) \end{align} $$ This is the form required to apply our proven method of solving for the unknown function \(f(t)\). Therefore, recasting the left side of our equality as $$ e^{\int P(t) dt} \frac{df}{dt} + P(t) e^{\int P(t) dt} f(t) = \frac{d}{dt}[e^{\int P(t) dt} f(t)] $$ the original equality can be rewritten as $$ \frac{d}{dt}[e^{\int P(t) dt} f(t)] = e^{\int P(t) dt} Q(t) $$ Integrating both sides, $$ \begin{align} \int \frac{d}{dt}[e^{\int P(t) dt} f(t)] dt &= \int e^{\int P(t) dt} Q(t) dt \\ e^{\int P(t) dt} f(t) &= \int e^{\int P(t) dt} Q(t) dt \\ f(t) &= \frac{1}{e^{\int P(t) dt}} \int e^{\int P(t) dt} Q(t) dt \end{align} $$

Conclusion

By using an integrating factor \(g(t)\), often written as \(µ(t)\), we’ve solved the ODE for the unknown function \(f(t)\). Therefore, if dealing with an ODE that can be algebraically cast as $$ \frac{df}{dt} + P(t)f(t) = Q(t) $$ we can use the method of integrating factors to solve for the unknown function \(f(t)\). Q.E.D
