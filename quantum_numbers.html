Quantum Numbers n, l, m (4/21/25)

Quantum Numbers n, l, m

\section{Introduction} The purpose of this blog is to derive the principal \(n\), orbital \(l\), and magnetic \(m\) quantum numbers from a rotationally invariant eigenvalue problem and to show how they underpin atomic orbitals. We assume that the reader has prerequisite knowledge of operator commutators, ladder operators, and eigenvalue basics. In Section 2 we setup the canonical particle in a box to derive \(n\); in Section 3 we introduce the notion of generators and symmetry through an example of translational invariance; in Section 4 we derive the rotationally invariant wavefunction; in Section 5 we relate \(n\), \(l\), and \(m\) to each other.

Infinite Potential Well

Infinite Potential Well

The total energy in a system is the sum of it's kinetic and potential energy \begin{equation} E = K + V \end{equation} In the case of a free particle moving through a system, we can think of the particle’s capacity to move through space in terms of the available kinetic energy \(K = E - V\). When the potential energy barrier is infinite, no finite amount of total energy (and therefore no finite kinetic energy) can ever exceed it. As a result, a free particle cannot penetrate or cross an infinite potential boundary. This situation is what defines an infinite potential well \begin{equation} V(x) = \begin{cases} 0, & \lvert x\rvert < \dfrac L2,\\[6pt] \infty, & \lvert x\rvert \ge \dfrac L2. \end{cases} \end{equation}

Derivation of the Principal Quantum Number \(n\)

The derivation of the principal quantum number leverages the continuity requirement to construct a system of two equations and two unknowns. We start with the second order differential equation \begin{equation} \frac{d^{2} \psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E - V)\psi = 0 \end{equation} Define \(\psi_{II}\) as the region where \(V(x) = 0\) and \(\psi_{I}, \psi_{III}\) as regions where \(V(x) = \infty\) and set up the system \begin{align} \frac{d^{2}}{dx^2}\psi_{I} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E - V)\psi_{I} = 0 \\ \frac{d^{2}}{dx^2}\psi_{II} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E)\psi_{II} = 0 \\ \frac{d^{2}}{dx^2}\psi_{III} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E - V)\psi_{III} = 0 \end{align} If we define \(k = \sqrt{\frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}}\), the solution form of \(\psi_{II}\) is \begin{align} \psi_{II} = Ae^{ikx} + Be^{-ikx} \end{align} Applying the continuity constraint at the boundaries of quadrant II and solving for \(k\) the trigonometric identity for \(\sin\) in terms of the \(e\) \begin{align} e^{-ikL} - e^{ikL} = -2i sin(kL) = 0 \end{align} holds if \(k = \frac{n \pi}{L}\) for \(n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots\). Plugging in for \(k\) and using a known trigonometric solution form for \(\psi_{II}\) the solution for the wave function at \(x\) given an integral value \(n\) is \begin{align} \psi_{n} &= (\frac{2}{L})^{\frac{1}{2}} \cos{(\frac{n \pi x}{L})} &\quad n &= 1, 3, 5, 7, \ldots \\ &= (\frac{2}{L})^{\frac{1}{2}} \sin{(\frac{n \pi x}{L})} &\quad n &= 2, 4, 6, \ldots \\ E_{n} &= \frac{\hbar^2 k_{n}^2}{2m} = \frac{\hbar^2 \pi^2 n^2}{2m L^2} \end{align} From \(E_{n}\), the principal quantum number, \(n\), directly labels the total energy level of our system.

An Introduction to Invariance: Translational Invariance \& Conservation of Momentum

Generator of Translations

A translation in classical mechanics moves the position of a particle from \(x\) to \(x + \epsilon\) and conserves momentum \(p\). In quantum mechanics, we tweak this definition to say that a translation moves the expected value of \(\langle X \rangle\) to \(\langle X \rangle + \epsilon\) and conserves the expected value of momentum \(\langle P \rangle\). If we define the original and translated states as \(|\psi\rangle\) and \(|\psi_{\epsilon}\rangle\), respectively, translation is written as \begin{align} \langle \psi_{\epsilon}| X | \psi_{\epsilon} \rangle &= \langle \psi | X | \psi \rangle + \epsilon \\ \langle \psi_{\epsilon}| P | \psi_{\epsilon} \rangle &= \langle \psi| P | \psi \rangle \end{align} We can deconstruct this further if we define the translation operator, \(T(\epsilon)\), as \begin{align} T(\epsilon)|\psi\rangle = |\psi_{\epsilon}\rangle \end{align} and rewrite the translation of the expected values of position and momentum as \begin{align} \langle \psi| T^{\dagger}(\epsilon) X T(\epsilon)| \psi \rangle &= \langle \psi | X | \psi \rangle + \epsilon \\ \langle \psi| T^{\dagger}(\epsilon) P T(\epsilon) | \psi \rangle &= \langle \psi| P | \psi \rangle \end{align} Immediate intuition would suggest the translation operator changes the state \(|x\rangle\) to \(|x + \epsilon\rangle\). However, the commutation relation \begin{align} [X, P] = i \hbar \end{align} only guarantees the structure of the position relative to the momentum operator, without specifying a phase. Accounting for this, our naive solution becomes \begin{align} T(\epsilon)|x\rangle = e^{\frac{i \epsilon g(x)}{\hbar}} |x + \epsilon \rangle \end{align} which reduces to the form \(T(\epsilon)|x\rangle = |x + \epsilon \rangle\) when \(g(x)\) is a constant. Now, we can use \(T(\epsilon)|x\rangle\) to explicitly construct the operator in order \(\epsilon\). The Taylor series expansion of \(T(\epsilon)\) about \(\epsilon = 0\) \begin{align} T(\epsilon) = I - \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar}G + O(\epsilon^{2}) \end{align} introduces the Hermitian generator of translations, \(G\). In other words, this generator is responsible for shifting the entire structure of the wavefunction by an infinitesimal amount. Another representation of the translation operator’s behavior is \begin{align} \langle x | T(\epsilon) | \psi \rangle = \psi(x - \epsilon) \end{align} where \(\psi(x - \epsilon)\) has a Taylor series expansion to \(\epsilon\) order of \begin{align} \psi(x - \epsilon) = \psi(x) - \frac{d \psi}{dx} \epsilon \end{align} Combining these two representations we arrive at the equality \begin{align} \langle x | I - \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar}G | \psi \rangle &= \psi(x) - \frac{d \psi}{dx} \epsilon \\ \langle x | I | \psi \rangle - \langle x | \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar}G | \psi \rangle &= \psi(x) - \frac{d \psi}{dx} \epsilon \\ \langle x | G | \psi \rangle &= -i \hbar \frac{d \psi}{dx} \end{align} From here, it is clear that \(G = P\) and our final form of the translation operator \(T(\epsilon)\) is \begin{align} T(\epsilon) = I - \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} P \end{align} This means that the momentum operator tells you how the entire state changes under an infinitesimal translation by an infinitesimal amount.

Translational Invariance

Translational invariance is when the expected value of a Hamiltonian is the same given an initial state \(|\psi\rangle\) and an infinitesimally translated state \(|\psi_{\epsilon}\rangle\). This is expressed as the equality \begin{align} \langle \psi | H | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi_{\epsilon} | H | \psi_{\epsilon} \rangle \end{align} Given that the generator of translation is the momentum operator \(P\), \begin{align} \langle \psi | H | \psi \rangle &= \langle \psi_{\epsilon} | H | \psi_{\epsilon} \rangle \\ &= \langle \psi| T^{\dagger}(\epsilon) H T(\epsilon)| \psi \rangle \\ &= \langle \psi | (I + \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} P)^\dagger H (I - \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} P) | \psi \rangle \\ &= \langle \psi | (I + \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} P) H (I - \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} P) | \psi \rangle \\ &= \langle \psi | H | \psi \rangle + \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} \langle \psi | PH - HP | \psi \rangle \\ &= \langle \psi | H | \psi \rangle + \frac{i \epsilon}{\hbar} \langle \psi | [P, H] | \psi \rangle \\ \end{align} For translational invariance to hold we require \([P, H] = 0\) which, by Ehrenfest’s theorem, tells us \(\langle \dot{P} \rangle\) = 0.
