Spin Angular Momentum of an Electron (2/17/25)

Empirically Arriving at Spin Angular Momentum

Spin angular momentum is best thought of as the internal angular momentum of a particle. The best way to illustrate this idea is through the experiment that led to an empirical discovery of spin. Researchers constructed a system such that the measurement of orbital angular momentum \(L_{x}\), \(L_{y}\), and \(L_{z}\) would yield zero. However, a measurement of the angular momentum about the z-axis yielded \(\pm \frac{\hbar}{2}\). This led to adding a component for spin angular momentum to the total angular momentum, \(\vec{J}\) of a system such that $$ \vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S} $$ where \(\vec{L}\) is the orbital angular momentum and \(\vec{S}\) is the spin angular momentum. Given that orbital angular momentum has three components \(L_{x}\), \(L_{y}\), and \(L_{z}\) we want to find the corresponding components of the spin angular momentum \(S_{x}\), \(S_{y}\), and \(S_{z}\) and the amount of components necessary for describing the electron's wave function.

Spin Angular Momentum Operators

In taking advantage of the following commutation relation of angular momentum, orbital angular momentum, and spin angular momentum $$ \begin{align} [J_{i}, J_{j}] &= i \hbar \sum_{k} \epsilon_{ijk} J_{k} \\ &= [L_{i}, L_{j}] + [S_{i}, S_{j}] \end{align} $$ Given the orbital angular momentum commutation relation \([L_{i}, L_{j}] = i \hbar \sum_{k} \epsilon_{ijk} L_{k}\), it’s clear that $$ [S_{i}, S_{j}] = i \hbar \sum_{k} \epsilon_{ijk} S_{k} $$ which is the same form as the commutation relation of the angular momentum \vec{J}. While the angular momentum matrices in \(J_{x}, J_{y}\), and \(J_{z}\) are infinite dimensional, we know they are block diagonal with each \((2j + 1) \times (2j + 1)\) obeying the commutation relation of \([S_{i}, S_{j}]\). With this connection between the structure of each block matrix and the spin angular momentum operators, the remaining step is to select one of the block matrices of the infinite set that sit on the diagonal. For this selection, we use our empirically measured spin angular momentum about the z-axis of \(\pm \frac{\hbar}{2}\) and match it to the block matrix related to \(j = \frac{1}{2}\) in units of \(\hbar\). Selecting for the equivalent angular momentum block matrices corresponding to \(j = \frac{1}{2}\) across \(J_{x}\), \(J_{y}\), and \(J_{z}\), we find the each spin angular momentum operator is a \(2 \times 2\) dimensional matrix $$ S_{x} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, S_{y} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, S_{z} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Spinor

The \(2 \times 2\) dimensionality of the \(\vec{S}\) suggests that the wave function of an electron, in the coordinate basis and about the z-axis, should have two components $$ \begin{align} \psi &= \begin{pmatrix} \psi_{+}(x,y,z) \\ \psi_{-}(x,y,z) \end{pmatrix} \\ &\equiv \psi_{+} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + \psi_{-} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$ where \(\psi_{+}\), \(\psi_{-}\) are the amplitudes of each eigenstate \(\chi_{+} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\chi_{-} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\) with eigenvalues of \(\frac{\hbar}{2}\), \(\frac{-\hbar}{2}\). By projecting the spin operators onto the vector \(\hat{n}\), $$ \begin{align} \hat{n} \cdot \vec{S} = n_{x} S_{x} + n_{y} S_{y} + n_{z} S_{z} \end{align} $$ solving the eigenvalue problem of the resulting matrix will yield the eigenstates $$ \begin{align} |\hat{n} +\rangle &= \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\frac{\theta}{2}) e^{-\frac{i \phi}{2}} \\ \sin(\frac{\theta}{2}) e^{\frac{i \phi}{2}} \end{pmatrix} \\ |\hat{n} -\rangle &= \begin{pmatrix} -\sin(\frac{\theta}{2}) e^{-\frac{i \phi}{2}} \\ \cos(\frac{\theta}{2}) e^{\frac{i \phi}{2}} \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$ These two possible eigenstates are often called spin up and spin down.

Pauli Matrices

The form of the operators \(S_{x}\), \(S_{y}\), and \(S_{z}\), we simplify the set of operators by introducing a linearly independent set of matrices called the Pauli matrices \(\vec{\sigma}\) which is defined by the equality $$ \vec{S} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \vec{\sigma} $$ or $$ \sigma_{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \sigma_{y} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \sigma_{z} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$ For purposes of completing the construction of a basis in the \(2 \times 2\) vector space we add $$ \sigma_{0} = I $$ These matrices have many useful properties however for the remainder of this blog we’ll focus on the property $$ (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{2} = I $$

Framing the Rotation Operator

The rotation operator is U[R(\vec{\theta}] = exp(-\frac{i \vec{\theta} \cdot \vec{S}}{\hbar})\). This operator generates a finite rotation \(\theta \hat{\theta}\) about the chosen \(\hat{n}\) axis. Since \(\vec{S} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \vec{\sigma}\), the rotation operator can be rewritten as $$ \begin{align} U[R(\vec{\theta})] &= exp(-i\frac{\vec{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma}}{2}) \\ &= exp[-i(\frac{\theta}{2}) \hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma}] \end{align} $$ Using the power series expansion of \(e^{x}\), $$ \begin{align} exp[-i(\frac{\theta}{2}) \hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma}] &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-\frac{i \theta}{2})^{n} \frac{1}{n!}(\hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n} \\ &= I + (-\frac{i \theta}{2}) \hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma} + \frac{1}{2!}(-\frac{i \theta}{2})^{2} (\hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{2} + \frac{1}{3!}(-\frac{i \theta}{2})^{3} (\hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{3} + … \end{align} $$

The Rotation Operator in Closed Form

Ordinarily, this would be intractable given that we’re dealing with increasing powers of infinite-dimensional spaces in the expansion. However, the property \((\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{2} = I\) allows us to reduce this into a sum of even and odd power coefficients. For \((\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n}\) an odd \(n\) reduces to $$ (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n} = (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})(\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n - 1} $$ Given the assumption that \(n\) is odd, \(n = 2k + 1\) and \(n - 1 = 2k\) which means we can rewrite our above representation as $$ (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n} = (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})(\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{2k} \\ $$ but \((\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{2k} = I\). Therefore, for an odd \(n\), $$ \begin{align} (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n} &= (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})I \\ &= (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma}) \end{align} $$ and an even \(n\), $$ (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{n} = I $$ As a direct result, the power series expansion can be written in the form $$ \begin{align} exp[-i(\frac{\theta}{2}) \hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma}] &= I + (-\frac{i \theta}{2}) \hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma} + \frac{1}{2!}(-\frac{i \theta}{2}) (\hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{2} + \frac{1}{3!}(-\frac{i \theta}{3}) (\hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma})^{3} + … \\ &= \cos(\frac{\theta}{2})I - i \sin(\frac{\theta}{2}) \hat{\theta} \cdot \vec{\sigma} \end{align} $$ which takes advantage of similar logic used to derive Euler’s identity of \(e^{i \theta/2} = \cos(\frac{\theta}{2}) + i\sin(\frac{\theta}{2})\). A closed form representation of the rotation operator in the spin basis allows us to confidently rotate the entire system by a finite angle \(\vec{\theta}\), while maintaining the inter-system properties (because \(U[R]\) is a unitary matrix).

Conclusion

Spin angular momentum of an electron can be measured in an arbitrary direction using a linear combination of the linearly independent Pauli matrices. These operators act on spinors which are two-component wave functions with amplitudes \(\psi_{\pm}\) and eigenvalues \(\pm \frac{\hbar}{2}\). These two quantum mechanical objects allow us to operate in the spin angular momentum basis and figure out the spin angular momentum contribution to an electrons overall angular momentum \(\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}\).
